Test Description

IMSE’s Phonological Awareness Diagnostic Assessment is designed to initially identify students who may be at risk for difficulty acquiring reading skills and to target areas for instruction. The assessment evaluates how efficiently and accurately a student can perceive, isolate, and manipulate sounds in spoken words—skills that are foundational for decoding, encoding, and overall reading development. The objective of this assessment is to identify strengths and areas of needed improvement, specifically in phonemic awareness. There are eleven subtests from the IMSE Phonological Awareness Diagnostic Assessment (Form A). See descriptions below.

  • Early Skills: Onset and Rime Blending
    Teddy was orally presented with six words segmented into onset-rime and asked to blend the sounds to form words (e.g., the teacher says /m/ /ăp/, the student says map).
  • Early Skills: First Sound Identification (CVC)
    Teddy was asked to isolate the first sound in six CVC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the first sound in map; the student says /m/).
  • Basic Skills: Last Sound Identification (CVC)
    Teddy was asked to isolate the last sound in six CVC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the last sound in map; the student says /p/).
  • Basic Skills: Middle Sound Identification (CVC)
    Teddy was asked to isolate the middle sound in six CVC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the middle sound in map; the student says /ă/).
  • Basic Skills: Phoneme Blending (CVC)
    Teddy was asked to blend the sounds in six CVC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to blend /n/ /ĕ/ /t/; the student says net).
  • Basic Skills: Phoneme Segmenting (CVC)
    Teddy was asked to isolate the three sounds in six CVC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the sounds in sat; the student says /s/ /ă/ /t/).
  • Advanced Skills: Phoneme Identification (CCVC and CVCC)
    Teddy was asked to identify the first, second, third, or last sound in six CCVC and CVCC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the first sound in block; the student says /b/).
  • Advanced Skills: Phoneme Blending (CCVC and CVCC)
    Teddy was asked to blend the sounds in six CCVC and CVCC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the word /k/ /l/ /ĭ/ /p/; the student says clip).
  • Advanced Skills: Phoneme Segmenting (CCVC and CVCC)
    Teddy was asked to state each sound in six CCVC and CVCC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the sounds in slug; the student says /s/ /l/ /ŭ/ /g/).
  • Advanced Skills: Phoneme Substitution (CVC, CCVC, and CVCC)
    Teddy was asked to change one of the sounds in six CVC, CCVC, and CVCC words to make a new word (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the word hot and then change the /h/ to /p/; the student says pot).
  • Advanced Skills: Phoneme Deletion (CVC, CCVC, and CVCC)
    Teddy was asked to take off a sound in a word to make a new word in six CVC, CCVC, and CVCC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the word pit and then say pit without /p/; the student says it).

Phonological Awareness Skills Test (PAST) (Concept of a Spoken Word, Rhyme, and Syllable only)

November 16, 2025

Test Description

The Phonological Awareness Skills Test evaluates a broad range of phonological awareness skills. Only three categories were assessed to obtain a broader understanding of Teddy’s phonological awareness skills. These categories identify specific gaps in phonological awareness at the word level. They are Concept of a Spoken Word, Rhyme, and Syllables. Descriptions of the categories and the specific skills are listed below.

Concept of a Spoken Word

  • Teddy was asked to identify the number of words in sentences containing three to five words, including a mix of single-syllable and multisyllabic words (e.g., the teacher says I like cake and the student says three).

Rhyme

  • Rhyme Recognition: Teddy was asked if two words rhyme (e.g., the teacher asks the student if the words sit and bit rhyme; the student says yes).
  • Rhyme Completion: Teddy was asked to choose a word that completes a rhyme pair (e.g., The teacher says I like to walk, I like to hike, I like to ride my big blue ___. The student says bike).
  • Rhyme Production: Teddy was asked to generate a rhyming word independently (e.g., the teacher asks the student to provide a word that rhymes with sit; the student states a rhyming word such as pit).

Syllables

  • Syllable Blending: Teddy was asked to combine given syllables to form a word (e.g., the teacher says out - side and the student says outside).
  • Syllable Segmentation: Teddy was asked to divide words into their syllable parts by saying and counting the number of syllables in each word (e.g., when given the word rainbow, the student identifies two syllables).
  • Syllable Deletion: Teddy was asked to say a word after removing a given syllable (e.g., the teacher asks the student to say sunshine without shine; the student says sun).

The task assesses processing speed, automatic retrieval, and integration of visual and verbal systems, all of which are critical for fluent reading. The colors version of the test was administered.

Acadience Reading - Grade 1/Benchmark 1

November 22, 2025

Acadience Reading is a research-based, standardized literacy assessment used to screen, diagnose risk, and monitor progress in early reading skills. It measures a student’s development in the foundational components of reading, including phonemic awareness, alphabetic principle, decoding, fluency, and comprehension.

Teddy was administered the following first grade Acadience benchmarks: Phoneme Segmentation, Letter Naming Fluency, and Nonsense Word Fluency. These assessments measure skills that a student in first grade should have at the beginning of the year.

Test Description

Nonsense Word Fluency - Grade 1/Benchmark 1

Test Description

Acadience Nonsense Word Fluency (NWF) measures how well a student can use their knowledge of letter sounds and phonics to read made-up (nonsense) words. It shows how accurately and automatically they can decode unfamiliar words, a key skill for developing fluent, real-word reading. The words are limited to consonant-vowel-consonant (CVC) and vowel-consonant (VC) patterns. The student is asked to read as many words as possible in one minute.

It is important to note that the Acadience benchmark did not include digraphs, even though they are taught in kindergarten (the IMSE Level 1 Initial Assessment included digraphs in some nonsense words). Teddy would benefit from decoding CVC words with greater accuracy and increased speed before introducing digraphs.

Teddy’s reading composite score (70) for the Acadience First Grade Benchmark (beginning of the year) shows that Teddy is well below benchmark (0-96) and is likely to need intensive support.

Given the accommodations provided to Teddy, the composite score of 70 is higher than it would be in a traditional school environment.                         

Conclusions

Teddy is a capable learner who is likely to make strong progress with an appropriate literacy foundation. It is recommended that he receive intensive, Orton-Gillingham–based reading instruction. Accommodations recommended by specialists and other professionals should be incorporated into his reading sessions to ensure he receives appropriate support.

Starting Points

1. Teddy will receive intensive, multi-sensory instruction, including: 

  • Explicit, direct instruction that is systematic (structured), sequential, and cumulative.
  • Instruction is organized and presented in a way that follows a logical sequential plan, fits the nature of language (alphabetic principle) with no assumption of prior skills or language knowledge, and maximizes student engagement. This instruction should proceed at a rate commensurate with his needs, ability levels, and demonstration of progress.
  • Intensive, highly concentrated instruction that maximizes student engagement, uses specialized methods and materials, and measures outcomes.
  • Meaning-based instruction that is directed toward purposeful reading and writing with an emphasis on comprehension and composition.
  • Multi-sensory instruction that incorporates the simultaneous use of two or more sensory pathways (auditory, visual, kinesthetic, tactile) during teacher presentations and student practice.

2. Teddy will receive explicit instruction and practice with decoding and encoding with the objective of achieving automaticity through the basic phoneme levels and accuracy at the more advanced levels.

3. Teddy will receive intensive vowel instruction with the objective of mastering short vowel sounds. The Vowel Intensive will be included until Teddy is successfully decoding and encoding short vowels in single-syllable words.

4. Reading instruction/review will begin with Concept #6 (Gg) with the goal to move through concepts strategically and quickly.

5. Spelling instruction will begin with Concept #6 (Gg).

6. Teddy will work on reading the words from the Red Words Level K with the goal of increasing automaticity.

7. Teddy will work towards spelling words from the Red Words Level K lists. Weekly spelling tests will be given to monitor progress.

Progress Monitoring Plan

It is recommended that progress monitoring of phoneme segmentation fluency, letter naming fluency, and nonsense word fluency should continue at the kindergarten level using Acadience or Dibels. Benchmarks should continue at grade level. Informal progress monitoring will occur weekly via IMSE spelling assessments of words and sentences (using CUPS) and timed repeated reading of the IMSE Decodable Readers, Rapid Word Charts and/or the Words and Sentences pages. Pause to Assess assessments and Midterm/Final Assessments will also be included.
