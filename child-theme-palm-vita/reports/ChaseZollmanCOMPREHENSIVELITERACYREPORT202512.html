IMSE Orton-Gillingham Phonological Awareness Diagnostic Assessment, Form A

November 29, 2025

Test Description

IMSE’s Phonological Awareness Diagnostic Assessment is designed to initially identify students who may be at risk for difficulty acquiring reading skills and to target areas for instruction. The assessment evaluates how efficiently and accurately a student can perceive, isolate, and manipulate sounds in spoken words—skills that are foundational for decoding, encoding, and overall reading development. The objective of the assessment is to identify strengths and areas of needed improvement, specifically in phonemic awareness. There are eleven subtests from the IMSE Phonological Awareness Diagnostic Assessment (Form A). See descriptions below.

  • Early Skills: Onset and Rime Blending
    Chase was orally presented with six words segmented into onset-rime and asked to blend the sounds to form words (e.g., the teacher says /m/ /ăp/, the student says map).
  • Early Skills: First Sound Identification (CVC)
    Chase was asked to isolate the first sound in six CVC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the first sound in map; the student says /m/).
  • Basic Skills: Last Sound Identification (CVC)
    Chase was asked to isolate the last sound in six CVC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the last sound in map; the student says /p/).
  • Basic Skills: Middle Sound Identification (CVC)
    Chase was asked to isolate the middle sound in six CVC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the middle sound in map; the student says /ă/).
  • Basic Skills: Phoneme Blending (CVC)
    Chase was asked to blend the sounds in six CVC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to blend /n/ /ĕ/ /t/; the student says net).
  • Basic Skills: Phoneme Segmenting (CVC)
    Chase was asked to isolate the three sounds in six CVC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the sounds in sat; the student says /s/ /ă/ /t/).
  • Advanced Skills: Phoneme Identification (CCVC and CVCC)
    Chase was asked to identify the first, second, third, or last sound in six CCVC and CVCC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the first sound in block; the student says /b/).
  • Advanced Skills: Phoneme Blending (CCVC and CVCC)
    Chase was asked to blend the sounds in six CCVC and CVCC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the word /k/ /l/ /ĭ/ /p/; the student says clip).
  • Advanced Skills: Phoneme Segmenting (CCVC and CVCC)
    Chase was asked to state each sound in six CCVC and CVCC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the sounds in slug; the student says /s/ /l/ /ŭ/ /g/).
  • Advanced Skills: Phoneme Substitution (CVC, CCVC, and CVCC)
    Chase was asked to change one of the sounds in six CVC, CCVC, and CVCC words to make a new word (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the word hot and then change the /h/ to /p/; the student says pot).
  • Advanced Skills: Phoneme Deletion (CVC, CCVC, and CVCC)
    Chase was asked to take off a sound in a word to make a new word in six CVC, CCVC, and CVCC words (e.g., the teacher asks the student to state the word pit and then say pit without /p/; the student says it).

Phonological Awareness Skills Test (PAST) (Concept of a Spoken Word, Rhyme, and Syllable only)

November 29, 2025

Test Description

The Phonological Awareness Skills Test evaluates a broad range of phonological awareness skills. Only three categories were assessed to obtain a broader understanding of Chase’s phonological awareness skills. These categories identify specific gaps in phonological awareness at the word level. They are Concept of a Spoken Word, Rhyme, and Syllables. These concepts should be mastered by kindergarten. Descriptions of the categories and the specific skills are listed below.

Concept of a Spoken Word

  • Chase was asked to identify the number of words in sentences containing three to five words, including a mix of single-syllable and multisyllabic words (e.g., the teacher says I like cake and the student says three).

Rhyme

  • Rhyme Recognition: Chase was asked if two words rhyme (e.g., the teacher asks the student if the words sit and bit rhyme; the student says yes).
  • Rhyme Completion: Chase was asked to choose a word that completes a rhyme pair (e.g., The teacher says I like to walk, I like to hike, I like to ride my big blue ___. The student says bike).
  • Rhyme Production: Chase was asked to generate a rhyming word independently (e.g., the teacher asks the student to provide a word that rhymes with sit; the student states a rhyming word such as pit).

Syllables

  • Syllable Blending: Chase was asked to combine given syllables to form a word (e.g., the teacher says out - side and the student says outside).
  • Syllable Segmentation: Chase was asked to divide words into their syllable parts by saying and counting the number of syllables in each word (e.g., when given the word rainbow, the student identifies two syllables).
  • Syllable Deletion: Chase was asked to say a word after removing a given syllable (e.g., the teacher asks the student to say sunshine without shine; the student says sun).

IMSE Orton-Gillingham Spell Sounds Foundational Skills Final Assessment

December 2, 2025

Test Description

The IMSE Orton-Gillingham Spell Sounds Foundational Skills Final Assessment measures a student’s ability to translate individual phonemes (sounds) into their corresponding graphemes (letters or letter combinations). During this assessment, the student is asked to write the correct letter(s) for each spoken sound. Thirty-six sounds are included in this assessment. This assessment evaluates early encoding skills, sound-symbol knowledge, and phoneme-grapheme association - essential foundation components for reading and spelling development within the Orton-Gillingham framework.

Starting Points

1. Chase will receive intensive, multi-sensory instruction, including: 

  • Explicit, direct instruction that is systematic (structured), sequential, and cumulative.
  • Instruction is organized and presented in a way that follows a logical sequential plan, fits the nature of language (alphabetic principle) with no assumption of prior skills or language knowledge, and maximizes student engagement. The instruction should proceed at a rate commensurate with his needs, ability levels, and demonstration of progress.
  • Intensive, highly concentrated instruction that maximizes student engagement, uses specialized methods and materials, and measures outcomes.
  • Meaning-based instruction that is directed toward purposeful reading and writing with an emphasis on comprehension and composition.
  • Multi-sensory instruction that incorporates the simultaneous use of two or more sensory pathways (auditory, visual, kinesthetic, tactile) during teacher presentations and student practice.

2. Chase will receive explicit instruction and practice with the following phonological skills: concept of a spoken word, rhyme production, and phoneme substitution (CVC, CCVC, and CVCC).

3. Chase will receive explicit instruction and practice with decoding and encoding with the objective of achieving automaticity through the basic phoneme levels and accuracy at the more advanced levels.

4. Chase will receive intensive vowel instruction with the objective of mastering short vowel sounds. The Vowel Intensive will be included until Chase is successfully decoding and encoding short vowels in single-syllable words.

5. Phonics instruction/review will begin at the middle of kindergarten with the goal to move through concepts strategically and quickly.

6. Spelling instruction will begin at the middle of kindergarten with the goal to move through concepts strategically and quickly.

7. Chase will work on reading the words from the Red Words Level 1 and 2 lists with the goal of increasing automaticity.

8. Chase will work towards spelling words from the Red Words Level K, 1, and 2 lists. Weekly spelling tests will be given to monitor progress.

9. Chase will work towards building greater fluency (accuracy and automaticity) with real words, nonsense words, and Red Words. He will also work towards building greater fluency at the word, phrase, and sentence level.

Pause to Assess assessments and Midterm/Final Assessments will also be included.
