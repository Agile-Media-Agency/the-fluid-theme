Fluid Dynamics

This isn't a theme with water names. It's a design system derived from the physics of how fluids actually behave.

The Realization

We started with water metaphors: Still, Serene, Trickling, Flowing, Rapids, Tsunami. Poetic names for motion intensities.

Then we tried to make the Rapids transition feel right. Not just "fast" - but physically correct. New content should push through old content. There should be friction. Momentum transfer. Displacement.

That's when it clicked: we weren't just naming things after water. We were accidentally implementing fluid mechanics.

"The theme isn't water-themed. It's physics-based. The water metaphors work because they describe real phenomena."

Core Principles

Every interaction in the Fluid Framework can be understood through fluid dynamics:

V Viscosity

Different elements have different "densities." Headers resist movement. Text flows freely. Old content is denser than new - it takes force to displace it.

D Displacement

When one element moves, it affects its neighbors. New content pushes old content out of the way. The old doesn't just disappear - it's displaced.

T Surface Tension

Elements have natural boundaries. On hover, buttons swell slightly before "breaking through" on click. There's resistance before the action.

P Pressure

Force applied to elements creates proportional response. Click a button and it compresses. The harder the press, the more it yields.

M Momentum

Objects in motion stay in motion. Transitions don't just stop - they settle. Overshoot, bounce back, find equilibrium. Like water finding its level.

F Friction

Movement through a medium encounters resistance. New content slows as it meets old content. Then it gains traction and accelerates.

The Reynolds Number

In fluid dynamics, the Reynolds number determines whether flow is smooth (laminar) or chaotic (turbulent). Our motion styles map directly to this spectrum:

Motion Style Flow Type Physical Behavior
Still No flow Zero energy. Absolute rest. Instant transitions.
Serene Laminar Smooth parallel layers. No mixing. Slow crossfades.
Trickling Low Re Viscosity dominates. Gentle, predictable movement.
Flowing Moderate Re Balanced forces. The comfortable default.
Rapids High Re Inertia dominates. Turbulent mixing. Content pushes through content.
Tsunami Extreme Boundary-breaking energy. Rise, crest, crash.

Rapids: A Case Study

The Rapids transition demonstrates true fluid dynamics:

0-15% Initial surge New content slowly emerges from below
15-35% Friction zone New meets old. Slows from resistance. Overlaps ~10%
35-40% Inertia lag Old content remains stationary. Dense fluid resists.
40-60% Momentum transfer New gains traction. Old begins to yield.
60-100% Displacement complete New pulls old away. Acceleration to final state.

This isn't arbitrary timing. It's what actually happens when one fluid displaces another.

Color as Depth

The Fluid palette maps motion to water color - not arbitrarily, but by depth and energy:

Still - Slate

Surface water reflecting an overcast sky. No movement, no color variance.

Serene - Soft Teal

A calm mountain lake. Clear enough to see depth, still enough to reflect.

Trickling - Light Cyan

A gentle stream catching sunlight. Shallow, playful, bright.

Flowing - Medium Blue

A steady river. Deep enough to be substantial, moving with purpose.

Rapids - Deep Teal

Whitewater. Aerated, energetic, mixing surface and depth.

Tsunami - Deep Navy

The Great Wave. Ocean depth meeting violent energy. Hokusai's blue.

Why This Matters

Design systems often feel arbitrary. "Use this easing curve because it looks nice." "This duration feels right."

Fluid Framework is different. Every timing, every easing curve, every interaction has a physical basis. It's not opinion - it's physics.

This gives us:

  • Internal consistency - Behaviors derive from the same principles
  • Intuitive feel - Users understand physics intuitively, even if they can't name it
  • Predictability - Given the motion style, you can predict how any element will behave
  • Extensibility - New components can derive their behavior from first principles
"Good design feels inevitable. Fluid dynamics makes it inevitable - because it's based on how the universe actually works."
